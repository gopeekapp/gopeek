Vertical Tabs vs Tab Groups: Which Actually Keeps You Organized?

June 14, 2026 · 7 min read · By GoPeek Team

Vertical tabs are better than tab groups. But that is like saying a bigger bucket is better when your house is flooding.

The horizontal tab bar was a mistake. It was designed in an era when people had three tabs open. Now the average knowledge worker has twenty-three, and the tab bar has become a ribbon of panic stretched across the top of the screen. You cannot read the titles. You cannot find the one tab you need. You just click randomly until the right favicon appears.

So the browser makers offered two solutions. Chrome and Edge gave us tab groups — color-coded folders for the panic ribbon. Firefox and Edge gave us vertical tabs — rotating the panic ninety degrees so it spills down the side of the screen instead of across the top.

Both are improvements. Neither is a solution. One arranges your chaos horizontally with highlights. The other arranges it vertically with scrollbars. The question is not which looks better. The question is which one actually helps you work — and whether either one is worth the effort.

What Tab Groups Actually Are

Tab groups are a confession. They are the browser admitting that the horizontal tab bar does not scale, but refusing to replace it. Instead, Chrome and Edge let you drag tabs into colored blobs. You can name the blobs. You can collapse them. You can turn a forty-tab disaster into a forty-tab disaster with a purple label.

The fundamental problem is geometry. A modern monitor is a widescreen rectangle. The horizontal tab bar gives you about twelve inches of width to display tabs. Each tab needs about an inch to show a readable title. That means your tab bar holds about twelve tabs before it starts scrolling. With tab groups, you now have scrolling blobs inside a scrolling bar. It is organizational origami. It is not organization.

And the labor is manual. Every new tab must be assigned. If you forget, it sits in the uncategorized wilderness at the end of the bar, slowly pushing your carefully grouped tabs into the overflow menu. You end up spending mental energy on tab taxonomy — is this Hacker News thread "Research" or "Random"? — instead of on your actual work.

The tab group illusion: Tab groups make you feel in control because you are doing something. You are sorting. You are labeling. You are color-coding. But the tab count is not dropping. The memory usage is not falling. The context switching is not stopping. You are just rearranging the deck chairs while the ship takes on water.

What Vertical Tabs Actually Are

Vertical tabs are the obvious fix. Monitors have more vertical space than horizontal space. A vertical sidebar can display thirty tab titles at a glance instead of twelve. You can read the full page name. You can nest them in trees. You can collapse branches. It is better in every measurable way.

Edge has a native vertical tabs feature. Firefox has Sidebery and Tree Style Tab. Chrome has nothing native — which tells you everything about Google's priorities — but extensions like Sidekick attempt to fill the gap.

Vertical tabs win on raw capacity. They win on readability. They win on screen geometry. If you are forced to choose between horizontal tab groups and a vertical tab tree, the vertical tree is the correct choice. It is not even close.

But here is the catch. Vertical tabs are still tabs. They are still browser processes. They still consume RAM. They still represent open loops in your working memory. They still require context switches. And they still encourage hoarding because the sidebar can hold so many tabs that you stop noticing how many you have open. A vertical tab bar with fifty tabs does not feel like fifty tabs. It feels like a tidy list. But your computer knows the truth. Your RAM knows the truth. And your focus knows the truth every time you Alt-Tab through a vertical stack of unrelated pages.

The Scenarios: Where Each One Breaks

The comparison table is useful, but scenarios reveal the real friction. Here is how tab groups and vertical tabs handle three common workflows.

Scenario 1: The Research Spiral (20+ tabs)

Tab groups: You open a Wikipedia article. It has twelve links. You open four in new tabs. Those four have their own links. You open three more. You try to group them by topic. You now have three groups with eight tabs each, and five ungrouped tabs that fell off the end of the bar. You cannot see the group names anymore because the bar is full. You give up and close the browser.

Vertical tabs: You open the same twelve links. The vertical sidebar shows all twenty tabs in a neat tree. You can see the titles. You can collapse branches. But your RAM is still at 3GB and climbing. Every tab is a live process. Your fan is spinning. And when you switch from the Wikipedia tab to the fifth nested PDF tab, you still lose your place in the original article. The tree is pretty. The context switching is real.

The truth: Neither system was designed for networked research. Both treat every link as a commitment. If you are opening twenty tabs, you do not have an organization problem. You have a tab creation problem.

Scenario 2: The Developer Context Switch

Tab groups: You have a "Docs" group, a "GitHub" group, a "Staging" group, and a "Slack" group. You are debugging an issue. You need to check the documentation, verify the PR, and test the staging site. You click through the groups. Each click is a context switch. Each switch costs you focus. The colors are nice, but your brain still has to unload the documentation mental model and load the staging environment mental model. The group color does not help.

Vertical tabs: You have the same four categories in a vertical tree. You can see all the titles. You can collapse the Slack branch so it stops flashing. But when you switch from the docs tab to the staging tab, the context switch is identical. The vertical layout does not reduce the cognitive cost. It just makes the tabs easier to find while you pay it.

The truth: Developers do not need better tab organization. They need fewer tabs. Documentation should be previewed, not opened. GitHub issues should be glanced at, not tabbed. The vertical tree makes a bad habit more sustainable. It does not eliminate the habit.

Scenario 3: The Casual Browser (5-10 tabs)

Tab groups: You have email, a news article, a YouTube video, and a shopping cart. You make a group for the shopping cart. It has one tab. You feel silly. You ungroup it. You have four tabs in a horizontal bar. You do not need groups. You need self-control.

Vertical tabs: You enable the vertical sidebar. It takes up 200 pixels of your screen to display four tabs. You have just sacrificed 15% of your monitor's width to solve a problem that did not exist. You disable it.

The truth: If you have fewer than ten tabs, both systems are overkill. The horizontal bar is fine. The real problems start at fifteen tabs, and neither system solves them. They just manage them more elegantly.

The Hidden Problem Both Share

Here is the uncomfortable truth that vertical tab enthusiasts and tab group apologists both ignore: the problem is not the orientation of the tabs. The problem is the quantity.

Vertical tabs are better than tab groups in the same way that a bigger closet is better than a smaller closet. If you own too many clothes, a bigger closet helps you find them. But it also encourages you to buy more clothes. The vertical sidebar is a bigger closet. It holds fifty tabs without breaking a sweat. So you open fifty tabs. And then you need tree structures, and collapse buttons, and search functions, and container integration, just to manage the wardrobe you have built.

Tab groups are the smaller closet with colored storage bins. You still have too many clothes. You just sorted them by hue. The bins are full. The floor is covered. But at least the chaos is categorized.

Neither system asks the fundamental question: Why do you have so many tabs open? The answer, for most knowledge workers, is that every link is a context switch they did not want to make. They open a new tab because they need to check a source, verify a claim, or read a reference — and they do not want to lose their current page. The tab is a safety net. The group is a safety net organizer. The vertical tree is a bigger safety net.

The real fix: If you could check every source, verify every claim, and read every reference without opening a new tab, you would not need tab groups or vertical tabs. You would need a preview layer. That is what GoPeek does — but we will get to that.

When Vertical Tabs Win

Vertical tabs are the correct choice if:

  • You are on Firefox with Sidebery or Tree Style Tab, and you genuinely need tree-structured browsing with container integration
  • You are on Edge and you want a native vertical sidebar without installing extensions
  • You regularly have 30+ tabs open for long-running projects, and you need to see all their titles at a glance
  • You use a ultrawide monitor where horizontal space is precious and vertical space is abundant
  • You are a developer or researcher who needs parent-child tab relationships automatically tracked

In these cases, vertical tabs are not just better than tab groups. They are essential. The horizontal bar is unusable at thirty tabs. Vertical tabs make it manageable. But manageable is not the same as good. It is just a less bad way to handle a bad habit.

When Tab Groups Win

Tab groups are the correct choice if:

  • You have 5-12 tabs open for a single, temporary session — like planning a trip or comparing products
  • You are on Chrome and refuse to install extensions or switch browsers
  • You need to share a group of tabs with someone else (Chrome allows tab group sharing in some contexts)
  • You are doing a presentation and want to color-code tabs for visual clarity while screen-sharing

That is a short list. Tab groups are a niche tool for light, temporary organization. They are not a system. They are a sticky note.

When Neither Wins

There is a third option that makes both tab groups and vertical tabs look like rearranging deck chairs. It is the option where you stop opening so many tabs in the first place.

If your workflow involves checking sources, verifying links, reading references, or comparing pages — the exact workflows that create tab overload — then you do not need a better tab system. You need a preview system. You need to be able to glance at a page without committing to it. You need to hover a link, see the content, and close it in five seconds without a new tab ever existing.

Vertical tabs are a better container. GoPeek is a smaller payload. If you are hauling fifty tabs, a bigger truck helps. But hauling fewer tabs helps more.

The Bottom Line

Vertical tabs beat tab groups. That is not an opinion. It is geometry. Monitors have more vertical space. Titles are readable. Trees are useful. The sidebar scales. If you are choosing between the two, choose vertical tabs every time.

But vertical tabs are still a container for a problem you should not have. They are a bigger bucket for the same flood. The real question is not whether your tabs should be horizontal or vertical. The question is why you need so many tabs that the orientation matters.

If you are a heavy researcher, a developer, a writer, or an analyst — someone who opens tabs because their work demands external references — then the answer is not a better tab bar. The answer is a tool that lets you access external references without opening tabs at all. Preview the link. Verify the source. Check the reference. Then close it and keep working. No tab. No group. No tree. No orientation required.

The verdict: Vertical tabs are better than tab groups. But a browser with fewer tabs is better than both. And a browser that previews instead of opening is better than fewer tabs.
