Vivaldi Web Panels vs GoPeek Previews

June 16, 2026 · 6 min read · By GoPeek Team

Persistent sidebar vs transient preview. Two different tools for two different jobs.

Vivaldi has a feature called Web Panels. It is a sidebar that holds persistent web pages. You can keep Twitter, your calendar, or your notes app open in a narrow panel while you browse. It is useful. It is well-designed. And it is completely different from what GoPeek does.

GoPeek previews are transient. You hover a link, a floating window opens, you read it, you close it. The preview does not stay open. It does not live in a sidebar. It appears when you need it and disappears when you do not.

Both tools show web content without using the main tab bar. But they solve different problems. Here is the direct comparison.

What Vivaldi Web Panels Actually Do

Web Panels are fixed sidebars inside Vivaldi. You add a URL to the panel list. It stays there permanently. You click the panel icon and the page loads in a narrow vertical strip on the left or right side of the browser. The panel stays open until you close it. It reloads when you restart the browser. It is a permanent fixture of your workspace.

Common uses for Web Panels:

  • Social media feeds (Twitter, Mastodon) kept visible while browsing
  • Messaging apps (WhatsApp Web, Telegram) always accessible
  • Email or calendar pinned to the side
  • Music players (Spotify Web, YouTube Music) with playback controls
  • Notes or todo lists (Notion, Todoist) visible during work

Web Panels are for tools you use constantly. They are not for checking a source link or verifying a citation. They are for keeping a secondary app visible at all times.

The downside: Web Panels consume RAM continuously. A Twitter panel uses 200-300MB whether you are looking at it or not. A Spotify panel uses 150MB. Add 4 panels and you are at 1GB of persistent RAM usage. The panels are always loaded. Always running. Always eating memory.

What GoPeek Actually Does

GoPeek is a link preview tool. You hold Shift and hover over any link. A floating window opens showing the linked page. You can scroll it, click inside it, and navigate it. When you move your mouse away or press Escape, it closes. The preview uses RAM only while it is open. When it closes, the memory is freed.

Common uses for GoPeek:

  • Checking a source link in an article without leaving the page
  • Verifying a ChatGPT citation without opening a tab
  • Comparing two product pages side-by-side with Multi-Peek
  • Referencing documentation while coding in sidebar mode
  • Previewing GitHub issues, Stack Overflow answers, or Reddit threads

GoPeek is for glances. It is for pages you need to check, verify, or reference briefly. It is not for apps you want permanently visible. The preview lives for seconds or minutes, not hours.

Three Scenarios

Scenario 1: Keeping Twitter Visible

Vivaldi: You add Twitter to your Web Panels. It sits on the left side of your browser. You see notifications. You check DMs. You scroll the feed while reading articles. The panel is always there. It uses 250MB of RAM continuously. You get used to it. You forget it is costing you.

GoPeek: Not the right tool. GoPeek does not keep pages open. If you want Twitter visible for hours, you need a sidebar or a tab. GoPeek is for checking a specific tweet, not monitoring a feed.

Winner: Vivaldi Web Panels. Persistent visibility is what they are built for.

Scenario 2: Verifying Sources in an Article

Vivaldi: You see a link to a research paper. You middle-click it. It opens in a new tab. You switch to the tab. You read the abstract. You switch back. You see another link. You middle-click again. Another tab. By the end of the article, you have 8 tabs. Your Web Panels are still sitting there, unused, eating 1GB of RAM.

GoPeek: You Shift-hover the first link. The preview opens. You read the abstract. You close it. You Shift-hover the second link. Same thing. You verify all 8 sources. Zero tabs created. Your Web Panels are irrelevant because you are not keeping any of these pages open.

Winner: GoPeek. Verification is a glance, not a permanent fixture.

Scenario 3: Comparing Two Product Pages

Vivaldi: You add both product pages to Web Panels. You can only see one panel at a time. You click the first panel. You see the specs. You click the second panel. You try to remember the specs from the first. You click back and forth. The panels are narrow. The pages are cramped. You give up and open both in tabs.

GoPeek: You preview the first product page. You drag it to the right side of your screen. You preview the second product page. You drag it to the left side. Both are visible simultaneously. You compare specs directly. You close both when done. No tabs. No cramped sidebar.

Winner: GoPeek. Side-by-side comparison needs space, not a narrow panel.

The Core Difference

Web Panels are for apps. GoPeek is for pages. Panels keep your secondary tools visible. Previews keep your primary workflow clean. They are not competitors. They are different categories.

The problem is that Vivaldi users sometimes try to use Web Panels for everything. They add a panel for every site they visit. The sidebar becomes crowded. The RAM usage climbs. The panels are too narrow for complex pages. The feature that was supposed to help becomes a burden.

GoPeek users have the opposite problem. They try to use previews for everything. They preview a page they actually need to work in for an hour. The preview is too small. They get frustrated. The tool that was supposed to help becomes limiting.

Use the right tool: Web Panels for apps you monitor constantly. GoPeek for pages you check briefly. Do not force one tool to do the other's job.

When to Use Vivaldi Web Panels

Use Web Panels if:

  • You already use Vivaldi and love the sidebar workflow
  • You want a social feed, messaging app, or music player visible at all times
  • You have 16GB+ RAM and can afford 1GB of persistent panel usage
  • You want a fixed workspace with predictable secondary tools

When to Use GoPeek

Use GoPeek if:

  • You verify sources, citations, or references frequently
  • You compare pages side-by-side
  • You reference documentation while working in another app
  • You want to keep your tab bar under 5 tabs
  • You use Chrome, Edge, or Firefox and do not want to switch browsers

The Browser Lock-In Problem

Web Panels only work in Vivaldi. If you use Vivaldi at home and Chrome at work, your panels do not transfer. Your workflow breaks. You have to rebuild your panel setup on every machine. If Vivaldi updates and breaks a panel, you wait for the fix.

GoPeek works in Chrome, Edge, and Firefox. Your previews work the same way on every machine. Your muscle memory transfers. Your workflow is consistent. If one browser has an issue, you switch to another and keep the same preview behavior.

This matters for teams. If your company requires Chrome for security, you cannot use Vivaldi Web Panels at work. You can use GoPeek.

The RAM Reality

Web Panels are not free. A typical setup with 4 panels (Twitter, WhatsApp, Spotify, Gmail) uses 800MB to 1.2GB of RAM continuously. That is RAM you pay for even when you are not looking at the panels. On an 8GB machine, that is 15% of your total memory gone before you open a single tab.

GoPeek uses RAM only when a preview is open. A typical preview uses 50-100MB. It closes. The memory is freed. Over a day of heavy use, GoPeek might use 500MB total, but never all at once. The peak is low. The average is near zero.

If you are RAM-constrained, Web Panels are expensive. GoPeek is cheap. The trade-off is convenience vs. efficiency.

What Actually Works

The best setup is hybrid. Use Vivaldi Web Panels for the 2-3 apps you genuinely need visible all day. Use GoPeek for everything else. Do not add 8 panels. Add 2. Preview the rest.

If you are not on Vivaldi, you do not need to switch browsers for panels. GoPeek's sidebar mode gives you a similar reference experience for the pages you need while working. It is not as permanent as a Web Panel, but it is more flexible and uses less RAM.

Verdict: Web Panels are for persistent apps. GoPeek is for transient pages. Use panels for your daily tools. Use previews for your daily links. Do not confuse the two.
